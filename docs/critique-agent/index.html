NanoClaw Classroom · Platform Capability Brief

A classroom where students build their own AI agents

A teaching platform in which each student authors and runs their own AI agent inside guardrails the instructor owns. This brief describes what the platform lets instructors and students do. A photography-critique unit appears at the end as one applied example.

§2
Roles & control

Two bounded roles: instructor sets the lines, student authors within them

The platform divides control cleanly. The instructor defines what is possible and required; each student decides who their agent is inside those bounds.

Instructor controls
  • The evaluative floor — the agent's stance, tone, conduct rules, and required output format. Authored once and propagated to every student.
  • The available tools — which skills students are permitted to enable.
  • Assessment — the rubric and a reference exemplar defining a strong build.
  • Administration — roster, per-student accounts, model and cost.
Each student authors
  • Identity — the agent's name, character, and voice.
  • Persona — how it works: its stance, what it prioritizes, and the questions it asks, in the student's own words.
  • Tool access — enabling or disabling the available skills and observing the effect on behavior.
  • Evaluation in use — running the agent on their own work and revising it until it is useful.
Why students build rather than consume. Specifying what an agent should ask forces a student to define what good work consists of. The exercise externalizes a developing vocabulary; the agent becomes an instrument for examining one's own judgment, not an authority that supplies answers.
§3
Persona stack

A locked floor, plus a layer that’s yours

The platform composes each agent’s persona from stacked layers (global → platform → class → per-agent). Every student inherits the locked floor, so even a weak or incomplete student persona still operates within the required bounds.

Platform & global
Locked. Base behavior shipped with the platform; the same for everyone.
Class base
Instructor-owned; locked to students. The evaluative floor — stance, conduct rules, required output format — edited once and propagated to every student agent.
My persona
Student-authored. The one layer each student writes and controls, in the Persona tab. Everything else is read-only to them.

§5
Extensible

Specialized companion agents

The build-your-own pattern is not limited to one task. Any agent a course needs can be authored the same way and added to the class library. One example, planned next: a studio-lighting reverse-engineer that takes an inspiration photograph and reconstructs a plausible production setup — a lighting diagram (key, fill, rim), modifiers and ratios, camera settings, lens, framing, and posing. Its text output pairs with the image-generation step (§6): the agent writes the setup and a ready-to-use generation prompt; the generator produces the visual reference.

§6
Image generation

Integrated image generation for previsualization

Agents can hand off to an image generator to previsualize a look — lighting, posing, framing, mood — before a live shoot. The platform is generator-agnostic; three options compared below trade off on access, lighting-control precision, photorealism, training-data provenance, and instructional value. Where an institution holds an OpenAI license with student credits, that materially shifts the comparison.

ChatGPT Images 2.0 (GPT Image 2) — OpenAI

Proposed primary tool

Reported (April 2026) as a reasoning-based generator on the GPT-5.4 backbone, producing up to 4K output and accepting reference images, with photorealism rated at the top of independent comparison leaderboards. Because it can reason over a brief and a reference image, it pairs directly with the companion lighting agent (§5): the agent can produce a setup description and a ready-to-use generation prompt. Where access is provided through an institutional OpenAI license and student credits, it removes the cost and account friction of consumer alternatives.

Considerations. The model post-dates this author's training data; capability claims are drawn from the sources in §11 and should be confirmed (§10). Training-data provenance and commercial-use terms are less transparent than Firefly's. Lighting control is prompt- and reference-driven rather than physically parameterized, so it is well suited to mood and overall look but less precise for isolating a single lighting variable.

Adobe Firefly — Adobe

Alternative — integrated and commercially safe

Defensible on grounds independent of raw photorealism. Firefly is trained on Adobe Stock and licensed or public-domain material, which Adobe positions as commercially safe — a meaningful consideration for coursework. It is typically available to students through their institutional accounts and is integrated into Photoshop (generative fill and expand), often a tool already taught, so it introduces no new application. Localized edits give moderate control over discrete changes such as adding a rim light or altering a background.

Considerations. Photorealism is generally rated below the current OpenAI model and below dedicated photorealistic models. Lighting control is prompt- and edit-based, not true relighting.

IC-Light + ControlNet on Stable Diffusion

Advanced — instructor-led lighting demonstration

Makers. Stable Diffusion is an open-weight model from Stability AI. ControlNet (Zhang, Rao & Agrawala, ICCV 2023) is an open-source method that conditions the model on structural inputs — human pose, depth, surface normals, edges — so subject and scene geometry can be fixed while other variables change. IC-Light ("Imposing Consistent Light," Lvmin Zhang, 2024) is an open-source relighting model from the same author.

Why it teaches lighting control. Unlike prompt-driven generators, IC-Light lets a student hold the subject constant and vary only the illumination — specifying light direction, quality, color temperature, and an implied source or background, and producing a consistent relit result. This makes light an isolated, manipulable variable, which maps directly onto the studio-lighting curriculum: a single portrait can be re-rendered as key-left versus key-right, with and without fill, with and without a rim or hair light, at high versus low ratio, and under warm versus cool sources. Students can compare canonical patterns — split, loop, Rembrandt, butterfly, broad and short lighting — and three-point setups side by side, on the same face, observing cause and effect in a way a live studio rarely permits within one session. ControlNet adds the complementary control of fixing pose and framing so that only lighting differs between comparisons.

Feasibility on available hardware. A dedicated diffusion station, separate from the classroom host, makes IC-Light practical. Two candidates: an NVIDIA DGX Spark (GB10, 128 GB) and a Mac Studio (M4 Max, 64 GB). IC-Light v1 is based on the lightweight Stable Diffusion 1.5, well within either machine's capacity. The two trade off differently. The DGX Spark is CUDA-native, so the open-source toolchain (ComfyUI, the ControlNet preprocessors, the IC-Light nodes, and their optimizations) installs and runs without the occasional gaps encountered under Apple's Metal backend; its 128 GB also allows substantially larger models (e.g. Flux) and heavy batching. Its memory bandwidth (273 GB/s), however, is the limiting factor for single-image latency, which is bandwidth-bound; the M4 Max, at roughly double that bandwidth, may render an individual SD 1.5 frame as quickly or quicker, at the cost of the Apple-Silicon toolchain caveats. With a dedicated station the principal constraints shift from compute to (1) toolchain setup and maintenance and (2) interactive throughput for a whole class at once. The practical role is therefore a shared or small-group generation station — by rotation, queue, or batch — rather than a per-student tool. A no-install browser demonstration remains available as a fallback (§11).

Recommended configuration. Adopt ChatGPT Images 2.0 as the primary previsualization tool, given institutional access and current image quality; retain Adobe Firefly as the integrated, commercially-safe alternative; and operate IC-Light with ControlNet on a dedicated diffusion station (the DGX Spark being the more compatible host) as a shared resource for controlled-relighting exercises. The three are complementary rather than competing, and the recommendation does not depend on any single vendor remaining ahead on image quality.
§7
Ownership

The instructor owns — and evolves — the platform

Ownership is more than administrative. The instructor can change the platform itself in plain language, without writing code, and every change is logged and reviewable. The agent a student chats with never gains write access; it only requests, and a separate operator process does the work on a branch.

1 · Ask

Describe it in plain language

e.g. “Add a skill that exports a critique conversation as a PDF.”

2 · Branch

An operator CLI makes the change

Claude Code (or Codex) implements it on a git branch and runs the build and tests.

3 · Review

Approve the diff as a Pull Request

GitHub’s PR view is the diff-review and approval gate; git history is the audit log.

4 · Deploy

Merge & redeploy; revert if needed

Pull, rebuild, restart. Rollback is a single git revert.

What the owner can ask for.
  • “Add a new persona to the class library: a studio-lighting reverse-engineer.”
  • “Let the lighting agent call the image generator from its setup prompt.”
  • “Tighten the class floor: never state exact settings — only ask the question that leads there.”
  • “Add a scheduled reminder to submit by Friday; raise the rate limit during the live lab.”
  • “Students on Safari can’t open the Playground link — please fix.”
Guardrails. Owner-only — never available to student or TA agents. Human approval of every diff before it lands; work happens on a branch, never the live read-only code; a build/test gate precedes redeploy; and security-critical paths (credentials, isolation, auth) are protected. A chat-driven trigger for the same pipeline is an optional later add-on.
§8
Runtime

Runs on your hardware

Host

Mac Studio (M1 Ultra, 32 GB). Orchestrates a short-lived container per chat message.

Models

Cloud-side (Claude via the Anthropic API) for the agents; the host does not run the language model.

Isolation

Each student agent runs in its own container; the project root is read-only to agents.

Local diffusion

Optional dedicated station for §6: DGX Spark (128 GB, CUDA-native) or M4 Max (64 GB). Shared/small-group use.

Portability

All materials are plain markdown; the exercise degrades to a self-built Claude Project if the platform is not deployed.

Export

Low-technology: the agent prints a summary in chat that the student retains. No export skill is built at this stage.

§9
In practice

An applied example: a photo-critique unit

One worked use of the platform — a one-week unit for an upper-level photography course. Students photograph a subject, then build a critique agent that questions their work Socratically rather than supplying fixes, and run it on their own images.

Authoring is a writing task — a seven-part scaffold

The unit gives students a guided scaffold (completed in the Persona tab) and a reflective worksheet that produces its content:

i

Identity

Name, character, and voice.

ii

Critique stance

Degree of directness; how far to question before offering a hint.

iii

Opening move

Greeting and the required technical-or-creative branch.

iv

Weighted criteria

Selecting and ranking composition, focus and depth of field, light, framing, moment, and background.

v

Question bank

The central task: two to three Socratic questions per criterion, posed rather than answered.

vi

Reshoot guidance

Helping the student select images to reshoot; producing the feedback summary.

vii

Handoff

Producing the submission summary the student retains.

Two lab sessions

Lab A — Construct and plan
  1. Draw a campus building at random.
  2. Introduction to the Playground; construct and test the critique agent on practice images.
  3. Enable and disable a skill; compare agent and unaided-model behavior.
  4. Prepare the fifty-image shot list.
Lab B — Critique and reshoot plan
  1. Import and cull to ten images in Lightroom Classic.
  2. Apply the student's own agent to the ten; work through the Socratic critique.
  3. Produce the feedback summary and submission record.
  4. Plan the four reshoots.

Between sessions: photograph the fifty images · After Lab B: reshoot the selected four
